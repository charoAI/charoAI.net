# Charles Babbage

Charles Babbage was an English mathematician, philosopher, and inventor born in 1791. For his designs of programmable calculating machines he is often described as the father of the computer.

Babbage held the post of Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at the University of Cambridge from 1828 to 1839, the chair once held by Isaac Newton, although he reportedly never delivered a lecture there.

His first great project was the Difference Engine, a mechanical calculator designed to produce error-free mathematical tables. The British government funded the Difference Engine, but the project stalled amid engineering difficulties and disputes with his chief engineer, and it was never finished in his lifetime.

In 1837 Babbage described the Analytical Engine, a far more ambitious general-purpose mechanical computer. Its design separated calculation into a mill that performed arithmetic and a store that held numbers, an arrangement that anticipated the processor and memory of modern computers.

Babbage collaborated closely with Ada Lovelace, who translated and annotated the fullest contemporary account of the Analytical Engine. He died in London in 1871. In 1991 the Science Museum in London completed Difference Engine No. 2 from his original drawings, and the finished machine worked exactly as Babbage had intended.
