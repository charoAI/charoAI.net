# Ada Lovelace

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, known to history as Ada Lovelace, was an English mathematician and writer born in London in 1815. She was the daughter of the poet Lord Byron and the mathematically gifted Anne Isabella Milbanke, who steered her daughter's education toward science and logic.

Lovelace was tutored by the Scottish scientist Mary Somerville, one of the most respected scientific writers of the era, and later studied logic and mathematics with Augustus De Morgan, a professor at University College London. Through Somerville she met Charles Babbage in 1833, and the two began a correspondence and working relationship that lasted for the rest of her life.

In 1842 the Italian engineer Luigi Menabrea published a short paper in French describing Babbage's proposed Analytical Engine. The following year Lovelace translated Menabrea's paper into English under the title "Sketch of the Analytical Engine". She appended her own notes, labelled A through G, which ran to roughly three times the length of the original paper.

The final note, Note G, describes a step-by-step method by which the Analytical Engine could compute Bernoulli numbers. Because it specifies a complete sequence of operations for a general-purpose machine, Note G is widely regarded as the first published computer program.

Lovelace saw further than most of her contemporaries. She argued that a machine that operated on symbols rather than only on numbers might one day compose music or produce graphics, an idea she called "poetical science". She died in 1852, at the age of thirty-six.
