# Analytical Engine

The Analytical Engine was a general-purpose mechanical computer designed by the English mathematician Charles Babbage. First described in 1837, it is considered the earliest design for a machine that could, in principle, perform any calculation.

The design borrowed its input mechanism from the Jacquard loom, a weaving machine invented by Joseph Marie Jacquard that used punched cards to control the pattern woven into fabric. In the Analytical Engine, both programs and data were to be supplied on punched cards.

Internally, the Engine separated computation into a mill, which performed arithmetic operations, and a store, which held up to one thousand numbers of forty decimal digits each. The design supported conditional branching and looping, the control structures that make a computer general-purpose rather than a mere calculator.

In 1842 the Italian engineer Luigi Menabrea, later a prime minister of Italy, published a description of the Analytical Engine in French, based on lectures Babbage had given in Turin. Ada Lovelace translated Menabrea's paper into English and added extensive notes of her own, including the method for computing Bernoulli numbers now celebrated as the first published computer program.

The Analytical Engine was never completed during Babbage's lifetime; Victorian engineering could not economically produce its thousands of precision parts. Its logical design, however, anticipated almost every element of the modern computer.
